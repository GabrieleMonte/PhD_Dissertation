## Chapter 1 — General Introduction

A broad narrative introducing the universe as a precision laboratory for beyond-the-Standard-Model (BSM) physics. Sets the stage for the three pillars of the thesis: primordial signals from inflation, dark matter and dark energy, and cosmic neutrinos. Introduces the key observables (CMB, LSS, 21-cm) and ends with a roadmap of the dissertation.

## Part II: Probing the Dark Universe with Cosmological and Terrestrial Observations

### Part II — Introduction
The dark sector — dark matter and dark energy — constitutes ~95% of the energy budget of the universe, yet its fundamental nature remains unknown. Both components leave imprints on cosmological observables and, in the case of DM, may also be within reach of terrestrial experiments. This Part explores multiple angles of attack: connecting cosmological DM production to direct detection prospects, constraining DM properties through CMB energy injection, testing non-particle DM candidates, and critically examining emerging evidence for dynamical dark energy.

### Chapter 8 — Minimal Dark Matter Freeze-in with Low Reheating Temperatures and Implications for Direct Detection
> K. Boddy, K. Freese, G. Montefalcone, B. Shams Es Haghi
> Phys.Rev.D 111 (2025) 6, 063537

### Chapter 9 — CMB Constraints on Loop-Induced Decays of Leptophilic Dark Matter
> G. Montefalcone, G. Elor, K. Boddy, N. Bellomo
> Phys.Rev.D 112 (2025) 2, 023506

### Chapter 10 — Does Memory Burden Open a New Mass Window for Primordial Black Holes as Dark Matter?
> G. Montefalcone, D. Hooper, K. Freese, C. Kelso, F. Kuhnel, P. Sandick
> Accepted to Phys.Rev.D (arXiv: 2503.21005)

### Chapter 11 — How Theory-Informed Priors Affect DESI Evidence for Evolving Dark Energy
> M. W. Toomey, G. Montefalcone, E. McDonough, K. Freese
> Submitted to Phys.Rev.D (arXiv: 2509.13318)

### Part II — Conclusions
The CMB and terrestrial experiments as complementary precision tools for constraining the dark sector — from particle DM production and decay, to non-particle DM candidates (PBHs), to the nature of dark energy. Highlights the guiding theme of using cosmological observations to provide both empirical constraints and critical guidance for theoretical exploration.

## Part III: Cosmological Probes of Neutrinos and Free-Streaming Radiation

### Part III — Introduction
Free-streaming radiation and the neutrino-induced phase shift: why isolating this signature matters for testing neutrino properties in a model-independent way. Overview of the phase-shift observable across different cosmological datasets.

> Accepted to Phys.Rev.D (arXiv: 2509.20363)

### Chapter 14 — Tracing the Neutrino-Induced Phase Shift in the 21-cm Spectrum
> G. Montefalcone, H. A. G. Cruz, J. B. Muñoz, E. D. Kovetz, M. Kamionkowski
> Submitted to Phys.Rev.D (arXiv: 2509.03595)

### Part III — Conclusions
The phase shift as a unified observable across CMB, BAO, and 21-cm — current constraints, future prospects with SO, and the path toward a comprehensive probe of the free-streaming nature of neutrinos.

## Chapter 15 — Conclusions and Outlook

Tie the three research threads together under the unifying theme of using cosmological observations to probe fundamental physics. Summarize key results from each Part. Lay out future directions: extending the phase-shift framework to broader BSM scenarios and LSS datasets, exploiting the 21-cm signal from cosmic dawn, secondary CMB anisotropies (SZ effect), and the ongoing work within the Simons Observatory.
